Business Validation

Core Metric: Unit Economics Health (LTV / CAC)

(Customer Lifetime Value / Customer Acquisition Cost)

Target: LTV:CAC ≥ 3:1

LTV Calculation Explained

LTV = (Average Purchase Value × Purchase Frequency) × Customer Lifespan

Example: A Small Restaurant

  • Avg. Purchase Value: RM400 per order
  • Purchase Frequency: 2 orders per month
  • Customer Lifespan: 12 months (estimated)

(RM400 × 2 orders/month) × 12 months = RM9,600 LTV

3. RFM Customer Segmentation: A Practical Approach

Use RFM (Recency, Frequency, Monetary) analysis to segment customers and tailor strategies. Customers who purchased recently (R), frequently (F), and spent more (M) are your most valuable assets.

How Scoring Works

Each customer is scored from 1 to 5 for each of the three categories. A score of 5 is always the best, resulting in a three-digit RFM score (e.g., 555 for your best customers).

Recency (R)

5 = Purchased very recently

1 = Purchased long ago

Frequency (F)

5 = Purchases very often

1 = Purchases rarely

Monetary (M)

5 = Spends a great deal

1 = Spends very little

4. Health Metrics: Watching for Hidden Risks

Note: While crucial for long-term sustainability, these metrics might be a secondary focus during the initial GTM phase (0-3 months) compared to core validation metrics like CAC and Activation Rate.

Customer Complaint Rate

Definition: Percentage of orders resulting in a formal complaint.

Warning: > 3%

Relevance: An early warning system for operational issues (delivery, quality, accuracy). High rates predict churn and damage brand reputation. Essential for protecting the premium JFB/Grab image.

Bad Debt Rate

Definition: Percentage of credit sales written off as uncollectible.

Warning: > 2%

Relevance: Directly impacts profitability and cash flow, especially critical in B2B with credit terms. High rates signal issues in credit checks, invoicing, or collections, risking financial stability during growth.

Category Penetration Rate

Definition: Average number of distinct product categories purchased per active customer.

Warning: < 3 avg.

Relevance: Measures customer "stickiness" and reliance on JFB. Low penetration means customers use us for niche items and are vulnerable to switching. High penetration indicates we are a primary supplier, increasing AOV and LTV.

5. Principles for Managing Metrics

  • Focus on "2+2" Core Metrics: Prioritize 2 strategic and 2 operational KPIs to avoid diluted focus.
  • Dynamically Adjust Weighting:
    • Months 0-3 (Validation): Focus on acquisition (CAC) & fulfillment.
    • Months 3-6 (Optimization): Shift to retention (repurchase rate) & LTV.
  • Beware of "Vanity Metrics": Avoid superficial data like 'registered users.' Focus on substantive metrics like 'paying customer ratio' and 'ARPU' that reflect true business health.

6. Strategic Cohesion & Our Right to Win

This section acts as a critical self-review to ensure the entire plan is cohesive, logical, and grounded in a sustainable competitive advantage.

How the Plan Connects: From Goals to Roadmap

Each part of this plan builds on the last, creating a clear path from high-level strategy to on-the-ground execution. The final revenue target is not a guess; it's the calculated outcome of successfully executing each step.

  1. Strategic Goals (e.g., Market Penetration) define WHAT we need to achieve. They are the high-level objectives.
  2. Operational KPIs (e.g., Customer Coverage Rate, CAC) are HOW we measure progress towards those goals. They make the strategy quantifiable.
  3. RFM Segmentation is the TOOL we use to apply our strategy effectively. It tells us WHO to target and with what specific actions (e.g., focus on acquiring "Potential Loyalists" in OC).
  4. The Roadmap is WHEN and WHERE we execute. It puts the goals, metrics, and segmentation into a timeline, allocating focus and resources (e.g., "Foundation" phase focuses on validating the OC acquisition playbook).

Direct Link Example: To hit the ~RM 16M target, we need a contribution from OC. This requires acquiring a base of accounts. The "Market Penetration" goal and its "Coverage Rate" KPI are the first, essential steps to building that customer base. Without penetration, there is no expansion revenue.

Critical Question: Is KV's "Deepen" Strategy Contradictory?

The plan includes acquisition for KV, which is a "Deepen" market. This is not a contradiction; it's a clarification of strategic focus. The difference between KV and OC is the *primary goal* of acquisition.

KV (Deepen)

Primary Goal: LTV Maximization. Grow revenue from the existing high-value base.

Acquisition Type: Strategic & high-quality. We hunt for a few, specific "lookalike" accounts that mirror our current Champions. It is quality over quantity.

OC (Widen)

Primary Goal: Market Penetration. Build a customer base from the ground up.

Acquisition Type: Scalable & high-volume. We build and refine a playbook to acquire many "Loyal" and "Potential" customers efficiently. It is quantity and process over individual account quality.

Our Right to Win: Why We Will Succeed

"Right to Win" defines our unique competitive advantages. Why will we succeed in a competitive B2B market?

  • Premium Brand & Trust: The Jaya Grocer brand is a powerful asset that resonates with high-value HORECA clients who prioritize quality and reliability over pure cost-cutting.
  • Superior Sourcing & Product Range: We offer a breadth and depth of SKUs, especially premium and imported goods, that traditional local wholesalers cannot match. This is key to increasing order value.
  • Technology & Logistical Synergy: Leveraging Grab's technology stack for last-mile delivery, data analytics, and operational efficiency provides a significant competitive advantage in fulfillment and customer intelligence.
  • Financial Backing: As part of a larger, well-capitalized entity, we have the resources to invest in a robust GTM strategy, absorb initial losses, and scale effectively where smaller players cannot.

Critical Risks & Roadblocks for Team Awareness (Revised for RM 16M Target)

  • Unit Economics Risk: With a lower overall scale, achieving the target LTV:CAC ratio (≥3:1) becomes even more critical. Inefficient acquisition or low retention could prevent profitability.

8. Long-Term Vision: The "3G Era" Ecosystem

These are long-term, potential strategies (not for immediate action) to strengthen the JFB business proposition and fully integrate into the Grab ecosystem, creating a powerful competitive moat.

G #1: Gxbank

Allow clients to earn GRP on all JFB purchases, which they can then redeem for other benefits within the Grab ecosystem (rides, food, etc.).

Actively cross-sell JFB supply solutions to the existing GFB client base and, conversely, offer GFB services (e.g., corporate transport) to JFB clients.
